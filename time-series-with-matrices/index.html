30. Univariate Time Series with Matrix Algebra

30.1Overview¶

This lecture uses matrices to solve some linear difference equations.

As a running example, we’ll study a second-order linear difference equation that was the key technical tool in Paul Samuelson’s 1939 article Samuelson (1939) that introduced the multiplier-accelerator model.

This model became the workhorse that powered early econometric versions of Keynesian macroeconomic models in the United States.

You can read about the details of that model in .

(That lecture also describes some technicalities about second-order linear difference equations.)

In this lecture, we’ll also learn about an autoregressive representation and a moving average representation of a non-stationary univariate time series {yt}t=0T\{y_t\}_{t=0}^T{yt​}t=0T​.

We’ll also study a “perfect foresight” model of stock prices that involves solving a “forward-looking” linear difference equation.

We will use the following imports:

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from matplotlib import cm

# Custom figsize for this lecture
plt.rcParams["figure.figsize"] = (11, 5)

# Set decimal printing to 3 decimal places

30.2Samuelson’s model¶

Let t=0,±1,±2,…t = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldotst=0,±1,±2,… index time.

For t=1,2,3,…,Tt = 1, 2, 3, \ldots, Tt=1,2,3,…,T suppose that

yt=α0+α1yt−1+α2yt−2y_{t} = \alpha_{0} + \alpha_{1} y_{t-1} + \alpha_{2} y_{t-2}yt​=α0​+α1​yt−1​+α2​yt−2​
(1)

where we assume that y0y_0y0​ and y−1y_{-1}y−1​ are given numbers that we take as initial conditions.

In Samuelson’s model, yty_tyt​ stood for national income or perhaps a different measure of aggregate activity called gross domestic product (GDP) at time ttt.

Equation (1) is called a second-order linear difference equation. It is called second order because it depends on two lags.

But actually, it is a collection of TTT simultaneous linear equations in the TTT variables y1,y2,…,yTy_1, y_2, \ldots, y_Ty1​,y2​,…,yT​.

Note

To be able to solve a second-order linear difference equation, we require two boundary conditions that can take the form either of two initial conditions, two terminal conditions or possibly one of each.

Let’s write our equations as a stacked system

[1000⋯000−α1100⋯000−α2−α110⋯0000−α2−α11⋯000⋮⋮⋮⋮⋯⋮⋮⋮0000⋯−α2−α11]⏟≡A[y1y2y3y4⋮yT]=[α0+α1y0+α2y−1α0+α2y0α0α0⋮α0]⏟≡b\underset{\equiv A}{\underbrace{\left[\begin{array}{cccccccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0\\ -\alpha_{1} & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0\\ -\alpha_{2} & -\alpha_{1} & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & -\alpha_{2} & -\alpha_{1} & 1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0\\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \cdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & -\alpha_{2} & -\alpha_{1} & 1 \end{array}\right]}}\left[\begin{array}{c} y_{1}\\ y_{2}\\ y_{3}\\ y_{4}\\ \vdots\\ y_{T} \end{array}\right]=\underset{\equiv b}{\underbrace{\left[\begin{array}{c} \alpha_{0}+\alpha_{1}y_{0}+\alpha_{2}y_{-1}\\ \alpha_{0}+\alpha_{2}y_{0}\\ \alpha_{0}\\ \alpha_{0}\\ \vdots\\ \alpha_{0} \end{array}\right]}}≡A⎣⎡​1−α1​−α2​0⋮0​01−α1​−α2​⋮0​001−α1​⋮0​0001⋮0​⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯⋯​0000⋮−α2​​0000⋮−α1​​0000⋮1​⎦⎤​​​⎣⎡​y1​y2​y3​y4​⋮yT​​⎦⎤​=≡b⎣⎡​α0​+α1​y0​+α2​y−1​α0​+α2​y0​α0​α0​⋮α0​​⎦⎤​​​
(2)

or

Ay=bA y = bAy=b
(3)

where

y=[y1y2⋮yT]y = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \cr y_2 \cr \vdots \cr y_T \end{bmatrix}y=⎣⎡​y1​y2​⋮yT​​⎦⎤​
(4)

Evidently yyy can be computed from

y=A−1by = A^{-1} by=A−1b
(5)

The vector yyy is a complete time path {yt}t=1T\{y_t\}_{t=1}^T{yt​}t=1T​.

Let’s put Python to work on an example that captures the flavor of Samuelson’s multiplier-accelerator model.

We’ll set parameters equal to the same values we used in .

T = 80

# parameters
α_0 = 10.0
α_1 = 1.53
α_2 = -.9

y_neg1 = 28.0 # y_{-1}
y_0 = 24.0

Now we construct AAA and bbb.

A = np.identity(T)  # The T x T identity matrix

for i in range(T):

    if i-1 >= 0:
        A[i, i-1] = -α_1

    if i-2 >= 0:
        A[i, i-2] = -α_2

b = np.full(T, α_0)
b[0] = α_0 + α_1 * y_0 + α_2 * y_neg1
b[1] = α_0 + α_2 * y_0

Let’s look at the matrix AAA and the vector bbb for our example.

A, b

Now let’s solve for the path of yyy.

If yty_tyt​ is GNP at time ttt, then we have a version of Samuelson’s model of the dynamics for GNP.

To solve y=A−1by = A^{-1} by=A−1b we can either invert AAA directly, as in

A_inv = np.linalg.inv(A)

y = A_inv @ b

or we can use np.linalg.solve:

y_second_method = np.linalg.solve(A, b)

Here make sure the two methods give the same result, at least up to floating point precision:

np.allclose(y, y_second_method)

AAA is invertible as it is lower triangular and its diagonal entries are non-zero

# Check if A is lower triangular
np.allclose(A, np.tril(A))
Note

In general, np.linalg.solve is more numerically stable than using np.linalg.inv directly. However, stability is not an issue for this small example. Moreover, we will repeatedly use A_inv in what follows, so there is added value in computing it directly.

Now we can plot.

plt.plot(np.arange(T)+1, y)
plt.xlabel('t')
plt.ylabel('y')

plt.show()

The *steady state* value y∗y^*y∗ of yty_tyt​ is obtained by setting yt=yt−1=yt−2=y∗y_t = y_{t-1} = y_{t-2} = y^*yt​=yt−1​=yt−2​=y∗ in (1), which yields

y∗=α01−α1−α2y^* = \frac{\alpha_{0}}{1 - \alpha_{1} - \alpha_{2}}y∗=1−α1​−α2​α0​​
(6)

If we set the initial values to y0=y−1=y∗y_{0} = y_{-1} = y^*y0​=y−1​=y∗, then yty_{t}yt​ will be constant:

y_star = α_0 / (1 - α_1 - α_2)
y_neg1_steady = y_star # y_{-1}
y_0_steady = y_star

b_steady = np.full(T, α_0)
b_steady[0] = α_0 + α_1 * y_0_steady + α_2 * y_neg1_steady
b_steady[1] = α_0 + α_2 * y_0_steady
y_steady = A_inv @ b_steady
plt.plot(np.arange(T)+1, y_steady)

30.3Adding a random term¶

To generate some excitement, we’ll follow in the spirit of the great economists Eugen Slutsky and Ragnar Frisch and replace our original second-order difference equation with the following second-order stochastic linear difference equation:

yt=α0+α1yt−1+α2yt−2+uty_{t} = \alpha_{0} + \alpha_{1} y_{t-1} + \alpha_{2} y_{t-2} + u_tyt​=α0​+α1​yt−1​+α2​yt−2​+ut​
(7)

where ut∼N(0,σu2)u_{t} \sim N\left(0, \sigma_{u}^{2}\right)ut​∼N(0,σu2​) and is IID, meaning independent and identically distributed.

We’ll stack these TTT equations into a system cast in terms of matrix algebra.

Let’s define the random vector

u=[u1u2⋮uT]u=\left[\begin{array}{c} u_{1}\\ u_{2}\\ \vdots\\ u_{T} \end{array}\right]u=⎣⎡​u1​u2​⋮uT​​⎦⎤​
(8)

Where A,b,yA, b, yA,b,y are defined as above, now assume that yyy is governed by the system

Ay=b+uA y = b + uAy=b+u
(9)

The solution for yyy becomes

y=A−1(b+u)y = A^{-1} \left(b + u\right)y=A−1(b+u)
(10)

Let’s try it out in Python.

σ_u = 2.
u = np.random.normal(0, σ_u, size=T)
y = A_inv @ (b + u)
plt.plot(np.arange(T)+1, y)
plt.xlabel('t')
plt.ylabel('y')

plt.show()

The above time series looks a lot like (detrended) GDP series for a number of advanced countries in recent decades.

We can simulate NNN paths.

N = 100

for i in range(N):

    u = np.random.normal(0, σ_u, size=T)

    plt.plot(np.arange(T)+1, y, lw=0.5, color=col)

plt.xlabel('t')
plt.ylabel('y')

plt.show()

Also consider the case when y0y_{0}y0​ and y−1y_{-1}y−1​ are at steady state.

N = 100

for i in range(N):
    col = cm.viridis(np.random.rand())  # Choose a random color from viridis

30.4Computing population moments¶

We can apply standard formulas for multivariate normal distributions to compute the mean vector and covariance matrix for our time series model

y=A−1(b+u).y = A^{-1} (b + u) .y=A−1(b+u).
(11)

You can read about multivariate normal distributions in this lecture Multivariate Normal Distribution.

Let’s write our model as

y=A~(b+u)y = \tilde A (b + u)y=A~(b+u)
(12)

where A~=A−1\tilde A = A^{-1}A~=A−1.

Because linear combinations of normal random variables are normal, we know that

y∼N(μy,Σy)y \sim {\mathcal N}(\mu_y, \Sigma_y)y∼N(μy​,Σy​)
(13)

where

μy=A~b\mu_y = \tilde A bμy​=A~b
(14)

and

Σy=A~(σu2IT×T)A~T\Sigma_y = \tilde A (\sigma_u^2 I_{T \times T} ) \tilde A^TΣy​=A~(σu2​IT×T​)A~T
(15)

Let’s write a Python class that computes the mean vector μy\mu_yμy​ and covariance matrix Σy\Sigma_yΣy​.

class population_moments:
    """
    Compute population moments μ_y, Σ_y.
    ---------
    Parameters:
    α_0, α_1, α_2, T, y_neg1, y_0
    """
    def __init__(self, α_0=10.0, 
                       α_1=1.53, 
                       α_2=-.9, 
                       T=80, 
                       y_neg1=28.0, 
                       y_0=24.0, 
                       σ_u=1):

        # compute A
        A = np.identity(T)

        for i in range(T):
            if i-1 >= 0:
                A[i, i-1] = -α_1

            if i-2 >= 0:
                A[i, i-2] = -α_2

        # compute b
        b = np.full(T, α_0)
        b[0] = α_0 + α_1 * y_0 + α_2 * y_neg1
        b[1] = α_0 + α_2 * y_0

        # compute A inverse
        A_inv = np.linalg.inv(A)

        self.A, self.b, self.A_inv, self.σ_u, self.T = A, b, A_inv, σ_u, T
    
    def sample_y(self, n):
        """
        Give a sample of size n of y.
        """
        A_inv, σ_u, b, T = self.A_inv, self.σ_u, self.b, self.T
        us = np.random.normal(0, σ_u, size=[n, T])
        ys = np.vstack([A_inv @ (b + u) for u in us])

        return ys

    def get_moments(self):
        """
        Compute the population moments of y.
        """
        A_inv, σ_u, b = self.A_inv, self.σ_u, self.b

        # compute μ_y
        self.μ_y = A_inv @ b
        self.Σ_y = σ_u**2 * (A_inv @ A_inv.T)
        
        return self.μ_y, self.Σ_y


series_process = population_moments()
    
μ_y, Σ_y = series_process.get_moments()
A_inv = series_process.A_inv

It is enlightening to study the μy,Σy\mu_y, \Sigma_yμy​,Σy​’s implied by various parameter values.

Among other things, we can use the class to exhibit how statistical stationarity of yyy prevails only for very special initial conditions.

Let’s begin by generating NNN time realizations of yyy plotting them together with population mean μy\mu_yμy​ .

for i in range(N):

    plt.plot(μ_y, color='red')

plt.xlabel('t')
plt.ylabel('y')

plt.show()

Visually, notice how the variance across realizations of yty_tyt​ decreases as ttt increases.

Let’s plot the population variance of yty_tyt​ against ttt.

Notice how the population variance increases and asymptotes.

Let’s print out the covariance matrix Σy\Sigma_yΣy​ for a time series yyy.

series_process = population_moments(α_0=0, 
                                    α_1=.8, 
                                    α_2=0, 
                                    T=6,
                                    y_neg1=0., 
                                    y_0=0.,

print("μ_y = ", μ_y)

Notice that the covariance between yty_tyt​ and yt−1y_{t-1}yt−1​ -- the elements on the superdiagonal -- are not identical.

This is an indication that the time series represented by our yyy vector is not stationary.

To make it stationary, we’d have to alter our system so that our initial conditions (y0,y−1)(y_0, y_{-1})(y0​,y−1​) are not fixed numbers but instead a jointly normally distributed random vector with a particular mean and covariance matrix.

We describe how to do that in Linear State Space Models.

But just to set the stage for that analysis, let’s print out the bottom right corner of Σy\Sigma_yΣy​.

series_process = population_moments()
μ_y, Σ_y = series_process.get_moments()

print("bottom right corner of Σ_y = \n", Σ_y[72:,72:])

Please notice how the subdiagonal and superdiagonal elements seem to have converged.

This is an indication that our process is asymptotically stationary.

You can read about stationarity of more general linear time series models in this lecture Linear State Space Models.

There is a lot to be learned about the process by staring at the off diagonal elements of Σy\Sigma_yΣy​ corresponding to different time periods ttt, but we resist the temptation to do so here.

30.5Moving average representation¶

Let’s print out A−1A^{-1}A−1 and stare at its structure

  • is it triangular or almost triangular or …\ldots… ?

To study the structure of A−1A^{-1}A−1, we shall print just up to 3 decimals.

Let’s begin by printing out just the upper left hand corner of A−1A^{-1}A−1.

print(A_inv[0:7,0:7])

Evidently, A−1A^{-1}A−1 is a lower triangular matrix.

Notice how every row ends with the previous row’s pre-diagonal entries.

Since A−1A^{-1}A−1 is lower triangular, each row represents yt y_tyt​ for a particular ttt as the sum of

  • a time-dependent function A−1bA^{-1} bA−1b of the initial conditions incorporated in bbb, and

  • a weighted sum of current and past values of the IID shocks {ut}\{u_t\}{ut​}.

Thus, let A~=A−1\tilde{A}=A^{-1}A~=A−1.

Evidently, for t≥0t\geq0t≥0,

yt+1=∑i=1t+1A~t+1,ibi+∑i=1tA~t+1,iui+ut+1y_{t+1}=\sum_{i=1}^{t+1}\tilde{A}_{t+1,i}b_{i}+\sum_{i=1}^{t}\tilde{A}_{t+1,i}u_{i}+u_{t+1}yt+1​=i=1∑t+1​A~t+1,i​bi​+i=1∑t​A~t+1,i​ui​+ut+1​
(16)

This is a moving average representation with time-varying coefficients.

Just as system (10) constitutes a moving average representation for yyy, system (9) constitutes an autoregressive representation for yyy.

30.6A forward looking model¶

Samuelson’s model is backward looking in the sense that we give it initial conditions and let it run.

Let’s now turn to model that is forward looking.

We apply similar linear algebra machinery to study a perfect foresight model widely used as a benchmark in macroeconomics and finance.

As an example, we suppose that ptp_tpt​ is the price of a stock and that yty_tyt​ is its dividend.

We assume that yty_tyt​ is determined by second-order difference equation that we analyzed just above, so that

y=A−1(b+u)y = A^{-1} \left(b + u\right)y=A−1(b+u)
(17)

Our perfect foresight model of stock prices is

pt=∑j=0T−tβjyt+j,β∈(0,1)p_{t} = \sum_{j=0}^{T-t} \beta^{j} y_{t+j}, \quad \beta \in (0,1)pt​=j=0∑T−t​βjyt+j​,β∈(0,1)
(18)

where β\betaβ is a discount factor.

The model asserts that the price of the stock at ttt equals the discounted present values of the (perfectly foreseen) future dividends.

Form

[p1p2p3⋮pT]⏟≡p=[1ββ2⋯βT−101β⋯βT−2001⋯βT−3⋮⋮⋮⋮⋮000⋯1]⏟≡B[y1y2y3⋮yT]\underset{\equiv p}{\underbrace{\left[\begin{array}{c} p_{1}\\ p_{2}\\ p_{3}\\ \vdots\\ p_{T} \end{array}\right]}}=\underset{\equiv B}{\underbrace{\left[\begin{array}{ccccc} 1 & \beta & \beta^{2} & \cdots & \beta^{T-1}\\ 0 & 1 & \beta & \cdots & \beta^{T-2}\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & \beta^{T-3}\\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{array}\right]}}\left[\begin{array}{c} y_{1}\\ y_{2}\\ y_{3}\\ \vdots\\ y_{T} \end{array}\right]≡p⎣⎡​p1​p2​p3​⋮pT​​⎦⎤​​​=≡B⎣⎡​100⋮0​β10⋮0​β2β1⋮0​⋯⋯⋯⋮⋯​βT−1βT−2βT−3⋮1​⎦⎤​​​⎣⎡​y1​y2​y3​⋮yT​​⎦⎤​
(19)
β = .96
# construct B
B = np.zeros((T, T))

for i in range(T):
    B[i, i:] = β ** np.arange(0, T-i)
print(B)
σ_u = 0.
u = np.random.normal(0, σ_u, size=T)
y = A_inv @ (b + u)
y_steady = A_inv @ (b_steady + u)
p = B @ y
plt.plot(np.arange(0, T)+1, y, label='y')
plt.plot(np.arange(0, T)+1, p, label='p')
plt.xlabel('t')
plt.ylabel('y/p')
plt.legend()

plt.show()

Can you explain why the trend of the price is downward over time?

Also consider the case when y0y_{0}y0​ and y−1y_{-1}y−1​ are at the steady state.

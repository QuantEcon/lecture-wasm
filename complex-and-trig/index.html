6. Complex Numbers and Trigonometry

6.1Overview¶

This lecture introduces some elementary mathematics and trigonometry.

Useful and interesting in its own right, these concepts reap substantial rewards when studying dynamics generated by linear difference equations or linear differential equations.

For example, these tools are keys to understanding outcomes attained by Paul Samuelson (1939) Samuelson (1939) in his classic paper on interactions between the investment accelerator and the Keynesian consumption function, our topic in the lecture Samuelson Multiplier Accelerator.

In addition to providing foundations for Samuelson’s work and extensions of it, this lecture can be read as a stand-alone quick reminder of key results from elementary high school trigonometry.

So let’s dive in.

6.1.1Complex Numbers¶

A complex number has a real part xxx and a purely imaginary part yyy.

The Euclidean, polar, and trigonometric forms of a complex number zzz are:

z=x+iy=reiθ=r(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)z = x + iy = re^{i\theta} = r(\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta})z=x+iy=reiθ=r(cosθ+isinθ)
(1)

The second equality above is known as Euler’s formula

  • Euler contributed many other formulas too!

The complex conjugate zˉ\bar zzˉ of zzz is defined as

zˉ=x−iy=re−iθ=r(cos⁡θ−isin⁡θ)\bar z = x - iy = r e^{-i \theta} = r (\cos{\theta} - i \sin{\theta} )zˉ=x−iy=re−iθ=r(cosθ−isinθ)
(2)

The value xxx is the real part of zzz and yyy is the imaginary part of zzz.

The symbol ∣z∣| z |∣z∣ = zˉ⋅z=r\sqrt{\bar{z}\cdot z} = rzˉ⋅z​=r represents the modulus of zzz.

The value rrr is the Euclidean distance of vector (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) from the origin:

r=∣z∣=x2+y2r = |z| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}r=∣z∣=x2+y2​
(3)

The value θ\thetaθ is the angle of (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) with respect to the real axis.

Evidently, the tangent of θ\thetaθ is (yx)\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)(xy​).

Therefore,

θ=tan⁡−1(yx)\theta = \tan^{-1} \Big( \frac{y}{x} \Big)θ=tan−1(xy​)
(4)

Three elementary trigonometric functions are

cos⁡θ=xr=eiθ+e−iθ2,sin⁡θ=yr=eiθ−e−iθ2i,tan⁡θ=yx\cos{\theta} = \frac{x}{r} = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2} , \quad \sin{\theta} = \frac{y}{r} = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i} , \quad \tan{\theta} = \frac{y}{x}cosθ=rx​=2eiθ+e−iθ​,sinθ=ry​=2ieiθ−e−iθ​,tanθ=xy​
(5)

6.2De Moivre’s Theorem¶

de Moivre’s theorem states that:

(r(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ))n=rneinθ=rn(cos⁡nθ+isin⁡nθ)(r(\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta}))^n = r^n e^{in\theta} = r^n(\cos{n\theta} + i \sin{n\theta})(r(cosθ+isinθ))n=rneinθ=rn(cosnθ+isinnθ)
(6)

To prove de Moivre’s theorem, note that

(r(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ))n=(reiθ)n(r(\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta}))^n = \big( re^{i\theta} \big)^n(r(cosθ+isinθ))n=(reiθ)n
(7)

and compute.

6.3Applications of de Moivre’s Theorem¶

6.3.1Example 1¶

We can use de Moivre’s theorem to show that r=x2+y2r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}r=x2+y2​.

We have

1=eiθe−iθ=(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)(cos⁡(-θ)+isin⁡(-θ))=(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)(cos⁡θ−isin⁡θ)=cos⁡2θ+sin⁡2θ=x2r2+y2r2\begin{aligned} 1 &= e^{i\theta} e^{-i\theta} \\ &= (\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta})(\cos{(\text{-}\theta)} + i \sin{(\text{-}\theta)}) \\ &= (\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta})(\cos{\theta} - i \sin{\theta}) \\ &= \cos^2{\theta} + \sin^2{\theta} \\ &= \frac{x^2}{r^2} + \frac{y^2}{r^2} \end{aligned}1​=eiθe−iθ=(cosθ+isinθ)(cos(-θ)+isin(-θ))=(cosθ+isinθ)(cosθ−isinθ)=cos2θ+sin2θ=r2x2​+r2y2​​
(8)

and thus

x2+y2=r2x^2 + y^2 = r^2x2+y2=r2
(9)

We recognize this as a theorem of Pythagoras.

(z,zˉ)(z, \bar z)(z,zˉ) form a complex conjugate pair of complex numbers.

(10)

6.3.3Example 3¶

This example provides machinery that is at the heard of Samuelson’s analysis of his multiplier-accelerator model Samuelson (1939).

Thus, consider a second-order linear difference equation

xn+2=c1xn+1+c2xnx_{n+2} = c_1 x_{n+1} + c_2 x_nxn+2​=c1​xn+1​+c2​xn​
(11)

whose characteristic polynomial is

z2−c1z−c2=0z^2 - c_1 z - c_2 = 0z2−c1​z−c2​=0
(12)

or

(z2−c1z−c2)=(z−z1)(z−z2)=0(z^2 - c_1 z - c_2 ) = (z - z_1)(z- z_2) = 0(z2−c1​z−c2​)=(z−z1​)(z−z2​)=0
(13)

has roots z1,z1z_1, z_1z1​,z1​.

A solution is a sequence {xn}n=0∞\{x_n\}_{n=0}^\infty{xn​}n=0∞​ that satisfies the difference equation.

Under the following circumstances, we can apply our example 2 formula to solve the difference equation

  • the roots z1,z2z_1, z_2z1​,z2​ of the characteristic polynomial of the difference equation form a complex conjugate pair

  • the values x0,x1x_0, x_1x0​,x1​ are given initial conditions

To solve the difference equation, recall from example 2 that

xn=2prncos⁡(ω+nθ)x_n = 2 pr^n \cos{(\omega + n\theta)}xn​=2prncos(ω+nθ)
(14)

where ω,p\omega, pω,p are coefficients to be determined from information encoded in the initial conditions x1,x0x_1, x_0x1​,x0​.

Since x0=2pcos⁡ωx_0 = 2 p \cos{\omega}x0​=2pcosω and x1=2prcos⁡(ω+θ)x_1 = 2 pr \cos{(\omega + \theta)}x1​=2prcos(ω+θ) the ratio of x1x_1x1​ to x0x_0x0​ is

x1x0=rcos⁡(ω+θ)cos⁡ω\frac{x_1}{x_0} = \frac{r \cos{(\omega + \theta)}}{\cos{\omega}}x0​x1​​=cosωrcos(ω+θ)​
(15)

We can solve this equation for ω\omegaω then solve for ppp using x0=2pr0cos⁡(ω+nθ)x_0 = 2 pr^0 \cos{(\omega + n\theta)}x0​=2pr0cos(ω+nθ).

With the sympy package in Python, we are able to solve and plot the dynamics of xnx_nxn​ given different values of nnn.

In this example, we set the initial values: - r=0.9r = 0.9r=0.9 - θ=14π\theta = \frac{1}{4}\piθ=41​π - x0=4x_0 = 4x0​=4 - x1=r⋅22=1.82x_1 = r \cdot 2\sqrt{2} = 1.8 \sqrt{2}x1​=r⋅22​=1.82​.

We first numerically solve for ω\omegaω and ppp using nsolve in the sympy package based on the above initial condition:

# Set parameters
r = 0.9
θ = π/4
x0 = 4
x1 = 2 * r * sqrt(2)

# Define symbols to be calculated
ω, p = symbols('ω p', real=True)

# Solve for ω
## Note: we choose the solution near 0
eq1 = Eq(x1/x0 - r * cos(ω+θ) / cos(ω), 0)
ω = nsolve(eq1, ω, 0)
ω = float(ω)
print(f'ω = {ω:1.3f}')

# Solve for p
eq2 = Eq(x0 - 2 * p * cos(ω), 0)
p = nsolve(eq2, p, 0)
p = float(p)
print(f'p = {p:1.3f}')

Using the code above, we compute that ω=0\omega = 0ω=0 and p=2p = 2p=2.

Then we plug in the values we solve for ω\omegaω and ppp and plot the dynamic.

6.3.4Trigonometric Identities¶

We can obtain a complete suite of trigonometric identities by appropriately manipulating polar forms of complex numbers.

We’ll get many of them by deducing implications of the equality

ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθe^{i(\omega + \theta)} = e^{i\omega} e^{i\theta}ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθ
(16)

For example, we’ll calculate identities for

cos⁡(ω+θ)\cos{(\omega + \theta)}cos(ω+θ) and sin⁡(ω+θ)\sin{(\omega + \theta)}sin(ω+θ).

Using the sine and cosine formulas presented at the beginning of this lecture, we have:

cos⁡(ω+θ)=ei(ω+θ)+e−i(ω+θ)2sin⁡(ω+θ)=ei(ω+θ)−e−i(ω+θ)2i\begin{aligned} \cos{(\omega + \theta)} = \frac{e^{i(\omega + \theta)} + e^{-i(\omega + \theta)}}{2} \\ \sin{(\omega + \theta)} = \frac{e^{i(\omega + \theta)} - e^{-i(\omega + \theta)}}{2i} \end{aligned}cos(ω+θ)=2ei(ω+θ)+e−i(ω+θ)​sin(ω+θ)=2iei(ω+θ)−e−i(ω+θ)​​
(17)

We can also obtain the trigonometric identities as follows:

cos⁡(ω+θ)+isin⁡(ω+θ)=ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθ=(cos⁡ω+isin⁡ω)(cos⁡θ+isin⁡θ)=(cos⁡ωcos⁡θ−sin⁡ωsin⁡θ)+i(cos⁡ωsin⁡θ+sin⁡ωcos⁡θ)\begin{aligned} \cos{(\omega + \theta)} + i \sin{(\omega + \theta)} &= e^{i(\omega + \theta)} \\ &= e^{i\omega} e^{i\theta} \\ &= (\cos{\omega} + i \sin{\omega})(\cos{\theta} + i \sin{\theta}) \\ &= (\cos{\omega}\cos{\theta} - \sin{\omega}\sin{\theta}) + i (\cos{\omega}\sin{\theta} + \sin{\omega}\cos{\theta}) \end{aligned}cos(ω+θ)+isin(ω+θ)​=ei(ω+θ)=eiωeiθ=(cosω+isinω)(cosθ+isinθ)=(cosωcosθ−sinωsinθ)+i(cosωsinθ+sinωcosθ)​
(18)

Since both real and imaginary parts of the above formula should be equal, we get:

cos⁡(ω+θ)=cos⁡ωcos⁡θ−sin⁡ωsin⁡θsin⁡(ω+θ)=cos⁡ωsin⁡θ+sin⁡ωcos⁡θ\begin{aligned} \cos{(\omega + \theta)} = \cos{\omega}\cos{\theta} - \sin{\omega}\sin{\theta} \\ \sin{(\omega + \theta)} = \cos{\omega}\sin{\theta} + \sin{\omega}\cos{\theta} \end{aligned}cos(ω+θ)=cosωcosθ−sinωsinθsin(ω+θ)=cosωsinθ+sinωcosθ​
(19)

The equations above are also known as the angle sum identities. We can verify the equations using the simplify function in the sympy package:

# Define symbols
ω, θ = symbols('ω θ', real=True)

# Verify
print("cos(ω)cos(θ) - sin(ω)sin(θ) =",
    simplify(cos(ω)*cos(θ) - sin(ω) * sin(θ)))
print("cos(ω)sin(θ) + sin(ω)cos(θ) =",
    simplify(cos(ω)*sin(θ) + sin(ω) * cos(θ)))

6.3.5Trigonometric Integrals¶

We can also compute the trigonometric integrals using polar forms of complex numbers.

For example, we want to solve the following integral:

∫−ππcos⁡(ω)sin⁡(ω) dω\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(\omega) \sin(\omega) \, d\omega∫−ππ​cos(ω)sin(ω)dω
(20)

Using Euler’s formula, we have:

∫cos⁡(ω)sin⁡(ω) dω=∫(eiω+e−iω)2(eiω−e−iω)2i dω=14i∫e2iω−e−2iω dω=14i(−i2e2iω−i2e−2iω+C1)=−18[(eiω)2+(e−iω)2−2]+C2=−18(eiω−e−iω)2+C2=12(eiω−e−iω2i)2+C2=12sin⁡2(ω)+C2\begin{aligned} \int \cos(\omega) \sin(\omega) \, d\omega &= \int \frac{(e^{i\omega} + e^{-i\omega})}{2} \frac{(e^{i\omega} - e^{-i\omega})}{2i} \, d\omega \\ &= \frac{1}{4i} \int e^{2i\omega} - e^{-2i\omega} \, d\omega \\ &= \frac{1}{4i} \bigg( \frac{-i}{2} e^{2i\omega} - \frac{i}{2} e^{-2i\omega} + C_1 \bigg) \\ &= -\frac{1}{8} \bigg[ \bigg(e^{i\omega}\bigg)^2 + \bigg(e^{-i\omega}\bigg)^2 - 2 \bigg] + C_2 \\ &= -\frac{1}{8} (e^{i\omega} - e^{-i\omega})^2 + C_2 \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \bigg( \frac{e^{i\omega} - e^{-i\omega}}{2i} \bigg)^2 + C_2 \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \sin^2(\omega) + C_2 \end{aligned}∫cos(ω)sin(ω)dω​=∫2(eiω+e−iω)​2i(eiω−e−iω)​dω=4i1​∫e2iω−e−2iωdω=4i1​(2−i​e2iω−2i​e−2iω+C1​)=−81​[(eiω)2+(e−iω)2−2]+C2​=−81​(eiω−e−iω)2+C2​=21​(2ieiω−e−iω​)2+C2​=21​sin2(ω)+C2​​
(21)

and thus:

∫−ππcos⁡(ω)sin⁡(ω) dω=12sin⁡2(π)−12sin⁡2(−π)=0\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(\omega) \sin(\omega) \, d\omega = \frac{1}{2}\sin^2(\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\sin^2(-\pi) = 0∫−ππ​cos(ω)sin(ω)dω=21​sin2(π)−21​sin2(−π)=0
(22)

We can verify the analytical as well as numerical results using integrate in the sympy package:

# Set initial printing
init_printing(use_latex="mathjax")

ω = Symbol('ω')
print('The analytical solution for integral of cos(ω)sin(ω) is:')
integrate(cos(ω) * sin(ω), ω)
print('The numerical solution for the integral of cos(ω)sin(ω) \
from -π to π is:')
integrate(cos(ω) * sin(ω), (ω, -π, π))

22. The Overlapping Generations Model

In this lecture we study the famous overlapping generations (OLG) model, which is used by policy makers and researchers to examine

  • fiscal policy

  • monetary policy

  • long-run growth

and many other topics.

The first rigorous version of the OLG model was developed by Paul Samuelson Samuelson (1958).

Our aim is to gain a good understanding of a simple version of the OLG model.

22.1Overview¶

The dynamics of the OLG model are quite similar to those of the Solow-Swan growth model.

At the same time, the OLG model adds an important new feature: the choice of how much to save is endogenous.

To see why this is important, suppose, for example, that we are interested in predicting the effect of a new tax on long-run growth.

We could add a tax to the Solow-Swan model and look at the change in the steady state.

But this ignores the fact that households will change their savings and consumption behavior when they face the new tax rate.

Such changes can substantially alter the predictions of the model.

Hence, if we care about accurate predictions, we should model the decision problems of the agents.

In particular, households in the model should decide how much to save and how much to consume, given the environment that they face (technology, taxes, prices, etc.)

The OLG model takes up this challenge.

We will present a simple version of the OLG model that clarifies the decision problem of households and studies the implications for long-run growth.

22.2Environment¶

We assume that time is discrete, so that t=0,1,…t=0, 1, \ldotst=0,1,….

An individual born at time ttt lives for two periods, ttt and t+1t + 1t+1.

We call an agent

  • “young” during the first period of their lives and

  • “old” during the second period of their lives.

Young agents work, supplying labor and earning labor income.

They also decide how much to save.

Old agents do not work, so all income is financial.

Their financial income is from interest on their savings from wage income, which is then combined with the labor of the new young generation at t+1t+1t+1.

The wage and interest rates are determined in equilibrium by supply and demand.

To make the algebra slightly easier, we are going to assume a constant population size.

We normalize the constant population size in each period to 1.

We also suppose that each agent supplies one “unit” of labor hours, so total labor supply is 1.

22.3Supply of capital¶

First let’s consider the household side.

22.3.1Consumer’s problem¶

Suppose that utility for individuals born at time ttt takes the form

Ut=u(ct)+βu(ct+1) U_t = u(c_t) + \beta u(c_{t+1})Ut​=u(ct​)+βu(ct+1​)
(1)

Here

  • u:R+→Ru: \mathbb R_+ \to \mathbb Ru:R+​→R is called the “flow” utility function

  • β∈(0,1)\beta \in (0, 1)β∈(0,1) is the discount factor

  • ctc_tct​ is time ttt consumption of the individual born at time ttt

  • ct+1c_{t+1}ct+1​ is time t+1t+1t+1 consumption of the same individual

We assume that uuu is strictly increasing.

Savings behavior is determined by the optimization problem

max⁡ct,ct+1 {u(ct)+βu(ct+1)} \max_{c_t, c_{t+1}} \, \left \{ u(c_t) + \beta u(c_{t+1}) \right \}ct​,ct+1​max​{u(ct​)+βu(ct+1​)}
(2)

subject to

ct+st≤wtandct+1≤Rt+1stc_t + s_t \le w_t \quad \text{and} \quad c_{t+1} \le R_{t+1} s_tct​+st​≤wt​andct+1​≤Rt+1​st​
(3)

Here

  • sts_tst​ is savings by an individual born at time ttt

  • wtw_twt​ is the wage rate at time ttt

  • Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​ is the gross interest rate on savings invested at time ttt, paid at time t+1t+1t+1

Since uuu is strictly increasing, both of these constraints will hold as equalities at the maximum.

Using this fact and substituting sts_tst​ from the first constraint into the second we get ct+1=Rt+1(wt−ct)c_{t+1} = R_{t+1}(w_t - c_t)ct+1​=Rt+1​(wt​−ct​).

The first-order condition for a maximum can be obtained by plugging ct+1c_{t+1}ct+1​ into the objective function, taking the derivative with respect to ctc_tct​, and setting it to zero.

This leads to the Euler equation of the OLG model, which describes the optimal intertemporal consumption dynamics:

u′(ct)=βRt+1u′(Rt+1(wt−ct)) u'(c_t) = \beta R_{t+1} u'( R_{t+1} (w_t - c_t))u′(ct​)=βRt+1​u′(Rt+1​(wt​−ct​))
(4)

From the first constraint we get ct=wt−stc_t = w_t - s_tct​=wt​−st​, so the Euler equation can also be expressed as

u′(wt−st)=βRt+1u′(Rt+1st) u'(w_t - s_t) = \beta R_{t+1} u'( R_{t+1} s_t)u′(wt​−st​)=βRt+1​u′(Rt+1​st​)
(5)

Suppose that, for each wtw_twt​ and Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​, there is exactly one sts_tst​ that solves (5).

Then savings can be written as a fixed function of wtw_twt​ and Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​.

We write this as

st=s(wt,Rt+1) s_t = s(w_t, R_{t+1})st​=s(wt​,Rt+1​)
(6)

The precise form of the function sss will depend on the choice of flow utility function uuu.

Together, wtw_twt​ and Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​ represent the prices in the economy (price of labor and rental rate of capital).

Thus, (6) states the quantity of savings given prices.

22.3.2Example: log preferences¶

In the special case u(c)=log⁡cu(c) = \log cu(c)=logc, the Euler equation simplifies to st=β(wt−st)s_t= \beta (w_t - s_t)st​=β(wt​−st​).

Solving for saving, we get

st=s(wt,Rt+1)=β1+βwt s_t = s(w_t, R_{t+1}) = \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} w_tst​=s(wt​,Rt+1​)=1+ββ​wt​
(7)

In this special case, savings does not depend on the interest rate.

22.3.3Savings and investment¶

Since the population size is normalized to 1, sts_tst​ is also total savings in the economy at time ttt.

In our closed economy, there is no foreign investment, so net savings equals total investment, which can be understood as supply of capital to firms.

In the next section we investigate demand for capital.

Equating supply and demand will allow us to determine equilibrium in the OLG economy.

22.4Demand for capital¶

First we describe the firm’s problem and then we write down an equation describing demand for capital given prices.

22.4.1Firm’s problem¶

For each integer t≥0t \geq 0t≥0, output yty_tyt​ in period ttt is given by the Cobb-Douglas production function

yt=ktαℓt1−α y_t = k_t^{\alpha} \ell_t^{1-\alpha}yt​=ktα​ℓt1−α​
(8)

Here ktk_tkt​ is capital, ℓt\ell_tℓt​ is labor, and α\alphaα is a parameter (sometimes called the “output elasticity of capital”).

The profit maximization problem of the firm is

max⁡kt,ℓt{ktαℓt1−α−Rtkt−wtℓt} \max_{k_t, \ell_t} \{ k^{\alpha}_t \ell_t^{1-\alpha} - R_t k_t -w_t \ell_t \}kt​,ℓt​max​{ktα​ℓt1−α​−Rt​kt​−wt​ℓt​}
(9)

The first-order conditions are obtained by taking the derivative of the objective function with respect to capital and labor respectively and setting them to zero:

(1−α)(kt/ℓt)α=wtandα(kt/ℓt)α−1=Rt (1-\alpha)(k_t / \ell_t)^{\alpha} = w_t \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha (k_t / \ell_t)^{\alpha - 1} = R_t(1−α)(kt​/ℓt​)α=wt​andα(kt​/ℓt​)α−1=Rt​
(10)

22.4.2Demand¶

Using our assumption ℓt=1\ell_t = 1ℓt​=1 allows us to write

wt=(1−α)ktα w_t = (1-\alpha)k_t^\alphawt​=(1−α)ktα​
(11)

and

Rt=αktα−1 R_t = \alpha k_t^{\alpha - 1}Rt​=αktα−1​
(12)

Rearranging (12) gives the aggregate demand for capital at time t+1t+1t+1

kd(Rt+1):=(αRt+1)1/(1−α) k^d (R_{t+1}) := \left (\frac{\alpha}{R_{t+1}} \right )^{1/(1-\alpha)}kd(Rt+1​):=(Rt+1​α​)1/(1−α)
(13)

In Python code this is

def capital_demand(R, α):
    return (α/R)**(1/(1-α))
def capital_supply(R, β, w):
    R = np.ones_like(R)
    return R * (β / (1 + β)) * w

(For the special case of log utility, supply does not depend on the interest rate, so we have a constant function.)

22.5Equilibrium¶

In this section we derive equilibrium conditions and investigate an example.

22.5.1Equilibrium conditions¶

In equilibrium, savings at time ttt equals investment at time ttt, which equals capital supply at time t+1t+1t+1.

Equilibrium is computed by equating these quantities, setting

s(wt,Rt+1)=kd(Rt+1)=(αRt+1)1/(1−α) s(w_t, R_{t+1}) = k^d(R_{t+1}) = \left (\frac{\alpha}{R_{t+1}} \right )^{1/(1-\alpha)}s(wt​,Rt+1​)=kd(Rt+1​)=(Rt+1​α​)1/(1−α)
(14)

In principle, we can now solve for the equilibrium price Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​ given wtw_twt​.

(In practice, we first need to specify the function uuu and hence sss.)

When we solve this equation, which concerns time t+1t+1t+1 outcomes, time ttt quantities are already determined, so we can treat wtw_twt​ as a constant.

From equilibrium Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​ and (13), we can obtain the equilibrium quantity kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​.

22.5.2Example: log utility¶

In the case of log utility, we can use (14) and (7) to obtain

β1+βwt=(αRt+1)1/(1−α) \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} w_t = \left( \frac{\alpha}{R_{t+1}} \right)^{1/(1-\alpha)}1+ββ​wt​=(Rt+1​α​)1/(1−α)
(15)

Solving for the equilibrium interest rate gives

Rt+1=α(β1+βwt)α−1 R_{t+1} = \alpha \left( \frac{\beta}{1+\beta} w_t \right)^{\alpha-1}Rt+1​=α(1+ββ​wt​)α−1
(16)

In Python we can compute this via

def equilibrium_R_log_utility(α, β, w):
    R = α * ( (β * w) / (1 + β))**(α - 1)
    return R

In the case of log utility, since capital supply does not depend on the interest rate, the equilibrium quantity is fixed by supply.

That is,

kt+1=s(wt,Rt+1)=β1+βwt k_{t+1} = s(w_t, R_{t+1}) = \frac{\beta }{1+\beta} w_tkt+1​=s(wt​,Rt+1​)=1+ββ​wt​
(17)

22.6Dynamics¶

In this section we discuss dynamics.

For now we will focus on the case of log utility, so that the equilibrium is determined by (17).

22.6.1Evolution of capital¶

The diagram shows that the model has a unique positive steady state, which we denote by k∗k^*k∗.

We can solve for k∗k^*k∗ by setting k∗=g(k∗)k^* = g(k^*)k∗=g(k∗), or

k∗=β(1−α)(k∗)α(1+β) k^* = \frac{\beta (1-\alpha) (k^*)^{\alpha}}{(1+\beta)}k∗=(1+β)β(1−α)(k∗)α​

22.7CRRA preferences¶

Previously, in our examples, we looked at the case of log utility.

Log utility is a rather special case of CRRA utility with γ→1\gamma \to 1γ→1.

In this section, we are going to assume that u(c)=c1−γ−11−γu(c) = \frac{ c^{1- \gamma}-1}{1-\gamma}u(c)=1−γc1−γ−1​, where γ>0,γ≠1\gamma >0, \gamma\neq 1γ>0,γ=1.

This function is called the CRRA utility function.

In other respects, the model is the same.

Below we define the utility function in Python and construct a namedtuple to store the parameters.

def crra(c, γ):
    return c**(1 - γ) / (1 - γ)

Model = namedtuple('Model', ['α',        # Cobb-Douglas parameter
                             'β',        # discount factor
                             'γ']        # parameter in CRRA utility
                   )

def create_olg_model(α=0.4, β=0.9, γ=0.5):
    return Model(α=α, β=β, γ=γ)

Let’s also redefine the capital demand function to work with this namedtuple.

def capital_demand(R, model):
    return (α/R)**(1/(1-model.α))

22.7.1Supply¶

For households, the Euler equation becomes

(wt−st)−γ=βRt+11−γ(st)−γ (w_t - s_t)^{-\gamma} = \beta R^{1-\gamma}_{t+1} (s_t)^{-\gamma}(wt​−st​)−γ=βRt+11−γ​(st​)−γ
(23)

Solving for savings, we have

st=s(wt,Rt+1)=wt[1+β−1/γRt+1(γ−1)/γ]−1 s_t = s(w_t, R_{t+1}) = w_t \left [ 1 + \beta^{-1/\gamma} R_{t+1}^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} \right ]^{-1}st​=s(wt​,Rt+1​)=wt​[1+β−1/γRt+1(γ−1)/γ​]−1

Notice how, unlike the log case, savings now depends on the interest rate.

def savings_crra(w, R, model):
    α, β, γ = model
    return w / (1 + β**(-1/γ) * R**((γ-1)/γ))

22.7.2Equilibrium¶

Equating aggregate demand for capital (see (13)) with our new aggregate supply function yields equilibrium capital.

Thus, we set

wt[1+β−1/γRt+1(γ−1)/γ]−1=(Rt+1α)1/(α−1) w_t \left [ 1 + \beta^{-1/\gamma} R_{t+1}^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} \right ]^{-1} = \left (\frac{R_{t+1}}{\alpha} \right )^{1/(\alpha - 1)}wt​[1+β−1/γRt+1(γ−1)/γ​]−1=(αRt+1​​)1/(α−1)

This expression is quite complex and we cannot solve for Rt+1R_{t+1}Rt+1​ analytically.

Combining (12) and (25) yields

kt+1=[1+β−1/γ(αkt+1α−1)(γ−1)/γ]−1(1−α)(kt)α k_{t+1} = \left [ 1 + \beta^{-1/\gamma} (\alpha k^{\alpha - 1}_{t+1})^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} \right ]^{-1} (1-\alpha)(k_t)^{\alpha}kt+1​=[1+β−1/γ(αkt+1α−1​)(γ−1)/γ]−1(1−α)(kt​)α

Again, with this equation and ktk_tkt​ as given, we cannot solve for kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​ by pencil and paper.

Solution to Exercise 1

To solve for kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​ given ktk_tkt​ we use Newton’s method.

Let

f(kt+1,kt)=kt+1[1+β−1/γ(αkt+1α−1)(γ−1)/γ]−(1−α)ktα=0 f(k_{t+1}, k_t) = k_{t+1} \left[ 1 + \beta^{-1/\gamma} \left ( \alpha k^{\alpha-1}_{t+1} \right )^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} \right] - (1-\alpha) k^{\alpha}_t =0f(kt+1​,kt​)=kt+1​[1+β−1/γ(αkt+1α−1​)(γ−1)/γ]−(1−α)ktα​=0
(27)

If ktk_tkt​ is given then fff is a function of unknown kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​.

Then we can use scipy.optimize.newton to solve f(kt+1,kt)=0f(k_{t+1}, k_t)=0f(kt+1​,kt​)=0 for kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​.

First let’s define fff.

def f(k_prime, k, model):
    α, β, γ = model.α, model.β, model.γ
    z = (1 - α) * k**α
    a = α**(1-1/γ)
    b = k_prime**((α * γ - α + 1) / γ)
    p = k_prime + k_prime * β**(-1/γ) * a * b
    return p - z

Now let’s define a function that finds the value of kt+1k_{t+1}kt+1​.

def k_update(k, model):
    return optimize.newton(lambda k_prime: f(k_prime, k, model), 0.1)

Solution to Exercise 2

We introduce a function hhh such that positive steady state is the root of hhh.

h(k∗)=k∗[1+β−1/γ(α(k∗)α−1)(γ−1)/γ]−(1−α)(k∗)α h(k^*) = k^* \left [ 1 + \beta^{-1/\gamma} (\alpha (k^*)^{\alpha-1})^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma} \right ] - (1-\alpha)(k^*)^{\alpha}h(k∗)=k∗[1+β−1/γ(α(k∗)α−1)(γ−1)/γ]−(1−α)(k∗)α
(29)

Here it is in Python

def h(k_star, model):
    α, β, γ = model.α, model.β, model.γ
    z = (1 - α) * k_star**α
    R1 = α ** (1-1/γ)
    R2 = k_star**((α * γ - α + 1) / γ)
    p = k_star + k_star * β**(-1/γ) * R1 * R2
    return p - z

Let’s apply Newton’s method to find the root:

k_star = optimize.newton(h, 0.2, args=(model,))
print(f"k_star = {k_star}")
